Analysis of Variance

Known as ANOVA, this analysis is used to compare the means of the variables for significant differences. One-way ANOVA, as its name may indicate, is for the comparison of means for one dependent variable, and MANOVA is for comparing variable means against those of more than one dependent variable. The ANOVA test is typically used to see if a procedure or effect has had any difference on the mean of the observations.

When performing an ANOVA, the null hypothesis is typically that all of the means are the same. You are seeking to disprove this by concluding that the means are significantly different, indicating that some action had a result on your data. Therefore, it follows that the alternative hypothesis is that at least one mean is statistically significant. As a general rule, a 95% percent confidence interval is a good measure, indicating that a p-value of .05 or less is required to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a statistically significant difference in the means.

Interpreting Two-way ANOVA / MANOVA Output

MATLAB® Functions for (M)ANOVA

  • [p,tbl,stats] = anova1(y) - this code will perform a one-way ANOVA and return the p-value, a table of descriptive statistics and a stats structure, which can be used as the MATLAB® function multcompare's input.
  • [p,tbl,stats] = anova2(y,reps) - will output the same results as above, but for a two-way ANOVA.
  • [d,p,stats] = manova1(X,group,alpha) - this will perform a MANOVA test, giving a p-value, p, an alpha value, d, and a stats structure. The alpha value is the significance level in terms of the dimensionality of the means.
